Day 2: TCP Handshake & TCP Stream Filter

Goal: Learn the TCP handshake and filter TCP traffic to analyze streams.

What I Learned:

The TCP handshake (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK) is the three-step process that establishes a connection between the client and server. This ensures reliable communication over the internet.

I learned to filter TCP traffic in Wireshark using the tcp filter and analyzed how data flows between a client and server using TCP streams.

TCP Streams allow for the tracking of data sent back and forth between devices. By following a TCP stream in Wireshark, it’s possible to see the entire communication, which is helpful in forensic investigations.

TCP Connection Lifecycle: The handshake process begins with a SYN packet, followed by a SYN-ACK from the server, and ends with the final ACK from the client. This ensures that both sides are ready to transmit and receive data reliably.


Reflection:

What went well? Filtering TCP traffic and following TCP streams worked better than I expected. I was able to easily see the full lifecycle of a TCP connection from start to finish.

What challenged you? Understanding the specific steps of the TCP handshake and why each step is necessary in establishing a connection was more complicated than I initially thought. I had to go over the process several times to grasp the full picture.

Key learning: The TCP handshake is the bedrock of reliable communication on the internet. By analyzing TCP streams, you can gain insight into the data exchange process between client and server, which is essential in tracking network activity.

Day 8: Port Scanning Patterns

Goal: Learn about port scanning patterns and identify SYN scans in PCAP files.

What I Learned:

Port scanning is a common reconnaissance technique used by attackers to identify open ports on a target system. This allows attackers to later exploit open services.

SYN scans (also called half-open scans) are used to detect open ports on a target system without establishing a full connection. This technique sends SYN packets to ports and listens for responses to determine if the port is open, closed, or filtered.

Wireshark helped me identify SYN scan patterns in PCAP files by looking for a large number of SYN packets without matching ACK responses. This pattern is characteristic of a SYN scan as the attacker never completes the handshake, avoiding full connection attempts that might be logged.

Recognized that port scanning is often the precursor to other attacks, such as exploitation of vulnerable services, and is typically followed by more targeted actions.


Reflection:

What went well? I was able to recognize SYN scan patterns in Wireshark logs and PCAP files by focusing on SYN packet behavior and their lack of follow-up ACK responses.

What challenged you? Understanding the subtle nature of SYN scans was tricky. SYN scans often mimic normal traffic, so it took some time to familiarize myself with recognizing the subtle differences in traffic patterns.

Key learning: Port scanning is a common precursor to more severe network attacks and can be detected by looking for patterns like SYN scans in traffic logs and PCAP files. Recognizing this is key in identifying malicious activity.

Goal: Learn the brute force attack pattern and detect repeated login attempts.

What I Learned:

A brute force attack occurs when an attacker attempts to gain unauthorized access by systematically trying many different passwords or keys, hoping to guess the correct one.

The pattern of a brute force attack is characterized by repeated failed login attempts that occur in quick succession, often from the same source IP address.

I learned how to detect these repeated login attempts in PCAP files by looking for patterns in authentication requests. By filtering for HTTP POST requests or SSH login attempts, I was able to track patterns indicating a brute force attack.

Wireshark helped me analyze login attempts and detect failed authentication by tracking the frequency and source of each attempt.

What challenged you? Differentiating legitimate traffic (e.g., regular login attempts) from a brute force attack required careful attention to the timing and frequency of the login attempts.

Key learning: Brute force attacks are typically characterized by high-frequency login attempts with different credentials in a short time frame. Recognizing this pattern is crucial for identifying unauthorized access attempts.



Day 10: Reverse Shells

Goal: Learn to detect reverse shells and suspicious outbound connections.

What I Learned:

A reverse shell is an attack where an attacker opens a command shell on a compromised system and connects back to their own machine to control it remotely.

Reverse shells create outbound connections from a compromised system to an attacker's machine, often using non-standard ports.

I learned to identify reverse shell traffic by looking for unusual outbound TCP connections to remote IP addresses, typically at non-standard ports (e.g., 443 or ports outside the usual web traffic ports).

By examining TCP streams, I identified signs of suspicious remote communication that could indicate a reverse shell. These connections often happen at irregular intervals, without apparent legitimate communication.


Reflection:

What challenged you? Differentiating reverse shell traffic from other remote access connections took time and patience, as legitimate remote desktop sessions can look similar.

Key learning: Reverse shells create outbound connections that can be detected by tracking unusual traffic patterns to remote IPs. These should be flagged for further investigation.

Day 15: C2 Beaconing Patterns

Goal: Learn about C2 (Command and Control) beaconing patterns and how to identify repeating intervals in PCAP files.

What I Learned:

C2 beaconing is a method attackers use to maintain communication with compromised systems. It involves the regular, repetitive communication between the compromised machine and the attacker's server, often at fixed intervals.

Beaconing patterns typically involve small, consistent bursts of traffic from the infected system to a remote server. These intervals may be used to maintain command-and-control (C2) channels for remote access or to exfiltrate data over time.

I learned how to detect C2 beaconing in PCAP files by looking for repetitive traffic with fixed time intervals. This includes analyzing packet times and identifying traffic that may not be part of regular network communications.

In Wireshark, I used filters to isolate specific types of beaconing patterns and detected them by examining the intervals between packets. I also looked for unusual IP addresses or ports, which often signify malicious activity.


Reflection:

What went well? I successfully identified C2 beaconing patterns by analyzing the packet timings and intervals. This helped me pinpoint potential malicious communication between a compromised machine and a remote server.

What challenged you? Recognizing beaconing in noisy traffic was tricky because C2 traffic often mimics normal communication patterns, making it difficult to differentiate from regular background traffic.

Key learning: C2 beaconing patterns are a strong indication of compromised systems. Being able to detect consistent traffic patterns at regular intervals is essential for identifying ongoing remote access or data exfiltration efforts.



Day 16: Encrypted Traffic Basics & TLS Handshake

Goal: Learn the basics of encrypted traffic and analyze TLS handshakes.

What I Learned:

TLS (Transport Layer Security) is a cryptographic protocol used to secure communication between client and server, ensuring confidentiality and integrity. TLS handshakes establish this secure channel by exchanging keys and agreeing on encryption algorithms.

The TLS handshake involves several steps, including client and server authentication, negotiation of encryption algorithms, and key exchange to establish a secure connection.

I learned how to analyze the TLS handshake in Wireshark and understand the various stages, from the client hello to the server hello, through to the final establishment of the secure session.

By analyzing the client hello and server hello packets, I could see the ciphers being negotiated and the session parameters being set up, which are crucial for identifying whether the encryption is being properly established or potentially tampered with.


Reflection:

What went well? I successfully analyzed the TLS handshake and understood its role in securely establishing a communication channel. This understanding helps in detecting issues like man-in-the-middle attacks or other encryption-related vulnerabilities.

What challenged you? The complexity of TLS encryption and the multiple stages of the handshake process made it challenging at first to fully understand the mechanics of secure communication.

Key learning: TLS handshakes provide valuable insight into the establishment of secure communication between a client and server. Understanding this process is essential for detecting malicious activity, especially if attackers attempt to exploit weak encryption setups.
